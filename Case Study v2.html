DFM Case Study

From cluster sprawl
to one control plane.

DFM is an enterprise control plane for Apache NiFi — a single place to deploy, govern, and monitor data flows across every cluster, every environment, and every team. We redesigned it around a question most platform teams can't answer by lunch: what's actually running, and is it healthy?

The project
at a glance.

DFM began as Ksolves' internal tool for wrangling Apache NiFi deployments across client environments. We rebuilt it as a multi-tenant SaaS for platform engineers, data ops leads, and compliance teams who live between NiFi canvases, audit logs, and a 2 AM pager.

NiFi is powerful.
Operating it
at scale is not.

NiFi ships a beautiful canvas for building flows. Everything around the canvas — multi-cluster deploys, RBAC, upgrades, schedules, audit — is a patchwork of shell scripts, REST calls, and tribal knowledge living in one senior engineer's head.

PAIN · 01

Cluster blindness

Teams managed 20–50 NiFi clusters across Dev, SAT, UAT, Staging and Prod. Health, version and RBAC lived in five different places. “Is Prod healthy?” took 4 tabs to answer.

PAIN · 02

Deploy-by-ticket

Promoting a process group from Dev to Prod meant export → Jira → manual import → pray. Median time-to-deploy: 3.4 days. 22% required a rollback.

PAIN · 03

Audit gaps

When a flow failed at 2 AM, the first hour was archaeology — grepping NiFi logs across nodes. Who deployed what, when, to which cluster? Nobody had a single answer.

“I don't need another NiFi. I need to know which one is lying.”
— Platform Lead, Fortune 500 logistics · discovery interview 07

What the
operators told us.

Four weeks of mixed-method research across eight customer environments and our own internal NiFi fleet. The pattern was sharp: operators didn't want a fancier canvas — they wanted the twenty minutes around it automated.

INSIGHT · 01

The list, not the canvas

Operators spent 80% of their day in lists — clusters, process groups, deployments — and 20% on the canvas. DFM had to optimize the 80%, not re-skin the 20%.

INSIGHT · 02

Trust is a UX problem

“Is this version the one actually running?” was the #1 recurring question. Versions, environments, and deploy status needed to be a first-class UI element, not a tooltip.

INSIGHT · 03

Audit, the first time

Every team rebuilt a homemade audit log out of NiFi logs + JIRA + Slack. A real activity history wasn't a compliance feature — it was the debugger operators had been missing.

13 — Reflections What we'd do next

What stuck,
what didn't.

A case study without regrets is a sales pitch. Three honest notes from the team retro, and three things we're taking forward into v2.

WHAT WORKED

A task-first IA.

Grouping the nav around Monitor / Operate / Catalog / Govern — with admin on a second shelf — out-tested every taxonomy we tried. Navigation errors dropped 71% in tree-testing.


Visible deploy history.

Making Activity History a first-class surface (not a tooltip) moved audit from a compliance chore to a core debugging tool. Compliance champions shipped in week one.

WHAT WE'D CHANGE

Shipped AI flow generation too late.

Users asked for a natural-language composer in week 2 of research. We shipped it in week 12. That gap cost us a month of compounding feedback and three signed LOIs.


Under-invested in onboarding.

DFM looks bleak on day one — empty clusters, empty registries. The v2 plan treats the first 10 minutes as a content problem, not a UI one: guided clusters, sample flows, a tour that doesn't feel like one.

14 — Scope & constraints What we could & couldn't change

Drawing the
box we played in.

Every honest case study lists what was out of scope. DFM ships into enterprise environments with tight constraints — air-gapped installs, LDAP, on-prem NiFi, and a hard "no black-box AI on customer data" line.

IN SCOPE · 14 WEEKS

What the redesign owned.

· Dashboard, Cluster, Process Group, Flow Registry, Deployment, Activity History and AI-Powered Flow surfaces.
· Full design system (tokens, components, icons).
· Mobile companion (iOS) for on-call.
· Interactive prototype for two usability rounds.

OUT OF SCOPE

Where we drew the line.

· The NiFi canvas itself — we kept the native Apache UI embedded, unmodified.
· Processor-level editing (handled by NiFi).
· Fine-grained RBAC UI (planned for v2).
· Customer-data previews inside the AI composer — legal blocker.

CONSTRAINT · 01

Air-gapped installs

No external fonts, CDNs, or telemetry. Design system shipped with self-hosted webfonts.

CONSTRAINT · 02

LDAP-first auth

Every role pattern had to map cleanly to existing enterprise LDAP groups.

CONSTRAINT · 03

NiFi API parity

DFM sits on top of the NiFi REST API — no custom endpoints that would block NiFi upgrades.

CONSTRAINT · 04

AI · safe defaults

Generated flows never auto-deploy. Every AI suggestion requires an operator diff approval.

16 — What's next DFM v2 roadmap

v2 is about
the first 10 minutes.

v1 won over the operators who already ran NiFi fleets. v2 has to win the next persona — the team just starting. Three bets, sequenced by the gaps we saw in launch telemetry.

01

Guided onboarding & sample flows

Day-one DFM is visually bleak. v2 ships a 10-minute guided setup — sample clusters, a starter flow registry, and an interactive tour that leaves a working pipeline behind.

Target · Q2 · closes new-user drop-off at day 2
02

Fine-grained RBAC editor

The flat "admin / editor / viewer" model didn't survive contact with real enterprise orgs. v2 introduces a visual permission matrix scoped by cluster, environment, and flow.

Target · Q3 · unblocks three F500 procurement reviews
03

AI flow generator · phase 2

Move from "prompt → draft processor group" to a conversational composer that reads the existing registry, suggests refactors, and explains diffs in plain English before every deploy.

Target · Q4 · ties AI to audit & diff review
